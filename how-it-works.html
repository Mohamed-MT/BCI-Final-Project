Introduction Applications Critical Discussion Recommendations

Medical application: Restoring communication

  • Implanted BCIs help individuals with ALS or spinal injuries control cursors, type messages, and operate robotic limbs using neural signals.
  • These systems translate brain activity into commands, enabling communication and basic environmental control.

Commercial development and human trials

  • Companies are testing implanted BCIs that allow users with paralysis to browse the internet, send texts, and interact with apps.
  • These trials show progress toward early consumer-facing systems rather than purely experimental prototypes.

Complexities and challenges

  • Invasive BCIs require surgery and bring risks such as infection, bleeding, and long-term complications.
  • Neural data are extremely sensitive, raising privacy concerns because they may reveal intentions or emotions.
  • Scar tissue can reduce implant performance over time, affecting reliability.
  • Cost and access create equity concerns, and media-driven expectations can pressure researchers and companies.
