Brain–Computer Interfaces
Introduction Applications Critical Discussion Recommendations

Social implications

  • BCIs change how people interact with technology and each other, offering independence and improved communication for some users.
  • They also raise questions about who controls neural data and how that data might be used, including concerns about surveillance or commercial exploitation.

Risks and accountability

  • Invasive BCIs involve surgical hazards, potential device failures, emotional stress for users, and the possibility of unintended outputs from misinterpreted neural signals.
  • These issues raise accountability questions about whether responsibility lies with users, clinicians, or device developers when devices behave unexpectedly.

Ethical lenses

  • Utilitarian: BCIs are ethical when benefits like restored communication and improved wellbeing outweigh harms, focusing on population-level outcomes.
  • Deontological: Emphasizes individual rights and duties, requiring informed consent, privacy protections, and limits on coercive use regardless of aggregate benefits.
